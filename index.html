Visualizing an Epidemic

May 1, 2020

Toy model for visualization purposes only, not an actual model for coronavirus.

Infection chance: 50%, n = 250, duration: 5s

As of the end of April, there are now 3.25 million coronavirus cases in the world, with over a million of those cases in the United States alone. Just two months ago, reported cases in the US were virtually non-existent, with less than 50 reported cases in the US. To get a better intuitive understanding of how such exponential growth occurs, it can be helpful to visualize a simplified model of how epidemics spread.

To begin, imagine we have a town of 250 healthy people, and one person becomes infected with a contagious disease. We must make some assumptions about how our disease spreads. For the sake of simplicity, let’s assume that there are three states for individuals -- healthy (susceptible), infectious, and removed (recovered or deceased). We will also assume that the disease has a chance of spread through close human to human proximity or contact, and that an infected individual will after a period of time will no longer be infectious by recovering or dying, removing them from the pool of infectious individuals. Finally we will assume that recovery confers a lasting resistance, so that they cannot become infected again, such as in the case of measles, mumps, and rubella. While these assumptions are far too simplistic to make for a real model, it should be sufficient for our goal of developing an intuition for spread.

In the example above, we started with a default infection chance of 50%, and a duration of 6 seconds. Let's see what happens if we cut the infection chance in half to 25%:

The first key insight we begin to see is that the speed of infection and amount of people infected are highly sensitive to both the duration and infection chance. We can think of this as diseases that have longer durations like the coronavirus spread much faster, whereas diseases with shorter durations like ebola (while having a high mortality rate) spread much slower. For infection chance, this can be thought of as a proxy for better hygiene, wearing of face masks, and overall caution.

What about social distancing and lockdown? Since one of our conditions of spread is human to human proximity or contact, let’s visualize what happens when we ask people to stay at home. If 100% of the population stops moving, the infection dies fairly quickly and isn’t able to spread, but presumably not everybody can simply stop all movement -- there are still essential workers, grocery trips, and other reasons why people won’t follow the lockdown order.

Try issuing the order to stay at home at various stages of the spread, and adjusting the percentage of people who are staying at home.

We can see that the earlier we issue the lockdown, the less spread there is. Also, lifting the lockdown too soon can cause a resurgence of cases, if the spread of infection is unmitigated.

Let’s also explore what happens when you issue a forced quarantine or travel restriction. Again for simplicity, we will proxy for this by introducing a simple divider that splits our population in half when we issue this restriction.
